The whole story, roughly

A guy who accidentally started a plugin company

Logan's a working musician and producer in modern heavy music — the kind of person who'll spend three hours tweaking a pitch-shift setting instead of sleeping. The Gross Button started as a dumb little trick he'd used in his own riffs for a decade. Eventually he figured, why keep it to himself? So now it's a plugin, and he's still a little surprised anyone else wants it.

Built from the studio, not a spec sheet

There's no roadmap, no investor deck, no focus group. Just Logan, stuck on a mix, muttering "there has to be a button for this" until eventually there was one.

Small, deliberate, and occasionally on time

The catalog's small on purpose — mostly because Logan is one guy with a guitar, not a plugin factory. Each release ships whenever he finally stops tweaking it, which is a real bottleneck.

What guides it (loosely)

Principles, not a roadmap

Made by someone who actually uses them

Every plugin starts the same way — Logan hits a wall in his own sessions, gets annoyed, and builds a weird little tool to get past it.

No interface bloat

Fewer knobs, fewer excuses. If Logan can't figure out a control by feel, it doesn't ship.

Heavy tones deserve serious tools

Djent, progressive metal, and modern heavy music push boundaries. The plugins should too — even if the branding doesn't take itself seriously.
